THE CPU

the Central Processing Unit

The central processing unit’s development make it one of the most important and influential artifacts in the field of computer science. Its history, evolution, and use highlight its impact on technology and computer science.

The leaps and bounds in progress seen over the last seventy years would not be possible without computers and the CPU gave them an edge to complete tasks much more efficiently.

HISTORY

From "fixed-program" to microprocessors


  • Early Computing Early machines, called Fixed-Program-Computers, required their hardware to be physically rewired to do different jobs.
  • Stored-Program computers stored instructions in high-powered memory rather than the computer having to be physically rewired. The ability to pre- program and store in memory all the instructions a computer would need to carry out computing tasks is the reason computers have been able to advance so much and so quickly.
  • Microprocessors The small chips that are today’s central processing units are microprocessors. The small-scale CPU was the biggest stepping stone to our modern microprocessors, and even boasted use in the Apollo Guidance Computer used by NASA.

THE CPU TODAY

Driving innovation and progress

Computers may be the most important driving force of our society. They facilitate growth, innovation and discovery in more ways than most of us can imagine. Without computers there would be no internet. Computers connect us in a way nothing else in history has. Computers are used to crunch numbers to predict economic growth and decline and serve as entertainment mediums in the form of videogame consoles, media streaming services, and social media platforms. Computer are used to run war simulations and to predict crime rates. They facilitate information exchange globally driving progress at an unprecedented accelerated rate. This technological age is what makes living in the twenty-first century so great.

And computers themselves wouldn’t be so great without the central processing unit using its logical instructions to execute any program we could possibly conceive of making. For these reasons I can’t think of a more important artifact than the central processing unit. The CPU is the brain of the computer, and thankfully it is a very smart one.
